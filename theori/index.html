Six-part framework

Each component targets a specific mechanism and should produce testable outputs.

1) Algorithmic Enhancement
observable
Filters systematically modify facial features (skin, geometry, lighting, symmetry). Output: a comparison target that is “close to self” but upgraded.
2) Actual–Digital Discrepancy
grounded
The gap between “actual self” and “digital self” is amplified via social value signals. Prediction: increased shame/dissatisfaction when facing the unfiltered self.
3) Perceptual Recalibration
hypothesis
With repetition, the filtered version may become the perceptual baseline. Prediction: reduced attractiveness ratings for unfiltered images after weeks of exposure.
4) Self‑Objectification
supported
Attention shifts from the whole self to “editable” parts. Prediction: increased appearance monitoring and behavioral reliance on editing.
5) Filter Dependence
hypothesis
Filters become an emotion-regulation tool and a safety behavior. Prediction: avoidance of calls/photos without filters; pre-posting anxiety.
6) Downstream Outcomes
mixed
Potential pathways to depression, social anxiety, and eating-disorder risk. Requires longitudinal designs and growth modeling.

    Fast execution roadmap

    If you want this to become an empirical paper, this is a sane path.

    Study 1: EMA + Model Fit
    8–12 weeks
    Daily: capture F(t), S(t), D(t). Then test β₃ with mixed-effects. Output: parameter estimates + threshold θ for recalibration.
    Study 2: EEG (LPP / FAA)
    ethics
    Compare filtered vs unfiltered self-images in ERP. Output: larger LPP for filtered; FAA shift during unfiltered (withdrawal).
    Study 3: fMRI Reward
    ROI
    Ventral striatum responds more to filtered self; response to unfiltered drops longitudinally. Output: evidence for “reward recalibration”.
    Intervention: Filter Abstinence
    RCT
    4-week abstinence + pre/post measures. Output: reduced discrepancy; increased body appreciation.
